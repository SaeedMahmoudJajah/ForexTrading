# Chapter 2 — The Drivers of EUR/USD

[← Anatomy of EUR/USD](01-anatomy.md) | [Volume 9 Home](README.md) | [Next: The Two Central Banks →](03-the-two-central-banks.md)

---

To trade EUR/USD with conviction, you must know exactly what moves it — and in what order of importance. This chapter applies the macro framework of Volume 6 specifically to EUR/USD, ranking its drivers from the dominant engine to the situational forces. Master this hierarchy and you'll understand *why* the pair moves, not just *that* it moved.

---

## 2.1 The hierarchy of EUR/USD drivers

```
1. MONETARY POLICY DIVERGENCE (Fed vs ECB)      ← THE ENGINE (Ch. 3)
2. THE INTEREST-RATE / YIELD DIFFERENTIAL        ← the market's real-time expression of #1
3. THE BROAD DOLLAR & RISK SENTIMENT             ← the dollar smile applied to EUR/USD
4. RELATIVE GROWTH (Eurozone vs US)              ← feeds the policy expectations
5. INFLATION (both sides)                        ← drives the central banks
6. EUROZONE-SPECIFIC RISK (fragmentation, energy, politics)
7. POSITIONING & EXPECTATIONS                    ← what's already priced in (Vol 6, Ch. 4)
```

> 🔑 Almost everything that moves EUR/USD flows through one question: **"What does this mean for the Fed-vs-ECB policy path, relative to what's priced in?"** (Vol 6, Ch. 2, 4). Data, growth, and inflation matter *because* they shift that policy expectation. Keep that lens and the drivers organize themselves.

---

## 2.2 Driver #1: Fed vs ECB monetary-policy divergence (the engine)

The single dominant driver of EUR/USD is the **relative stance of the two central banks** (full treatment in Ch. 3). Capital flows toward the currency offering the better, safer return (Vol 6, Ch. 2):

```
Fed MORE hawkish than the ECB (US rates rising/higher vs Eurozone)  →  EUR/USD DOWN
ECB MORE hawkish than the Fed (Eurozone catching up / out-hiking)   →  EUR/USD UP
Both shifting together                                              →  muted; watch the GAP
```

- It's the **divergence** (the gap between the two paths) that matters, not either bank alone.
- It's **expectations** that move the pair: a *change* in the expected gap, relative to what's priced (Vol 6, Ch. 4). A Fed turning unexpectedly dovish *narrows* the gap → EUR/USD up.
- Most multi-month EUR/USD trends are **"Fed vs ECB divergence" stories** (e.g. the 2021–22 dollar surge as the Fed hiked aggressively while the ECB lagged sent EUR/USD below parity).

> 🔑 This is the master driver. Your EUR/USD bias begins by comparing the two central banks' **policy paths relative to what's priced in** (Ch. 3; Vol 6, Ch. 7). Get the divergence right and you're aligned with the pair's primary force.

---

## 2.3 Driver #2: the yield differential (the real-time tell)

The policy divergence shows up **in the bond market before it fully shows up in EUR/USD** — making yield spreads a leading, real-time tell:

- Watch the **US Treasury vs German Bund** yield spread (Germany is the Eurozone's benchmark sovereign). The **2-year spread** is especially policy-sensitive; the **10-year spread** captures the broader picture.
- **US yields rising faster than German yields** (widening spread in the dollar's favor) → **EUR/USD down**; the reverse → **EUR/USD up**.
- This is the cleanest **intermarket confluence** for the pair (Vol 6, Ch. 6): a EUR/USD direction backed by a matching move in the US–German yield spread is higher-conviction.

> 🔑 The US–German yield differential is EUR/USD's most useful "second chart." It often **leads** the pair, because bond markets price policy expectations fast. If EUR/USD is falling *and* the US–German spread is widening, the move has a solid macro engine behind it.

---

## 2.4 Driver #3: the broad dollar & risk sentiment (the dollar smile)

Because the dollar is half the pair (and ~58% of DXY), **broad dollar behavior drives EUR/USD heavily** — and the dollar's behavior follows the **dollar smile** (Vol 8, Ch. 2; Vol 6, Ch. 5):

```
Applied to EUR/USD:
  • GLOBAL RISK-OFF / panic     → dollar bid as a haven      → EUR/USD DOWN (left of smile)
  • US ECONOMY OUTPERFORMS      → dollar bid for yield        → EUR/USD DOWN (right of smile)
  • US MUDDLES while world grows → dollar weak, risk-on       → EUR/USD UP (middle of smile)
```

- **EUR/USD tends to RISE in calm, risk-on "middle of the smile" conditions** (dollar soft, capital seeking growth/risk) and **FALL when the dollar is bid** — whether from fear (risk-off) or US outperformance.
- The euro itself is a **semi-cyclical** currency: it tends to do better when global growth is healthy and worse in crises (when the dollar's haven status dominates).

> 🔑 Always ask **which side of the dollar smile** you're on. A falling EUR/USD driven by *fear* (risk-off, dollar haven) behaves differently from one driven by *US strength* (right of the smile) — and a rising EUR/USD usually means a calm, risk-on, soft-dollar world. The cause shapes what to expect next.

---

## 2.5 Driver #4: relative growth (Eurozone vs US)

Growth differentials feed the policy expectations and capital flows:

- **US growth outperforming the Eurozone** → stronger dollar, hawkish-Fed expectations → **EUR/USD down.** **Eurozone outperforming** → **EUR/USD up.**
- The key data: **PMIs** (especially the German and Eurozone flash PMIs — leading indicators), **GDP**, and activity data on both sides (Vol 6, Ch. 3; full calendar in Ch. 4).
- For years a recurring theme has been **US economic outperformance vs a sluggish Eurozone**, a structural headwind for EUR/USD — but these cycles turn, and catching the turn is where macro edge lives (Vol 6).

---

## 2.6 Driver #5: inflation (both sides)

Inflation drives the central banks, so it's a top-tier EUR/USD mover (Vol 6, Ch. 3):

- **US inflation** (CPI, **PCE** — the Fed's preferred gauge) drives Fed expectations.
- **Eurozone inflation** (**HICP** — the harmonized index the ECB targets, plus German CPI) drives ECB expectations.
- **Relative inflation surprises** move the divergence: hotter US inflation → hawkish Fed → EUR/USD down; hotter Eurozone inflation → hawkish ECB → EUR/USD up.

> 🔑 EUR/USD reacts to **inflation surprises on *both* sides** — US CPI/PCE and Eurozone HICP are among the biggest scheduled movers of the pair, because each reshapes its respective central bank's path (Ch. 4).

---

## 2.7 Driver #6: Eurozone-specific risks (the euro's own story)

Most of the time EUR/USD is a Fed-vs-ECB-and-dollar story. But the euro has unique vulnerabilities that occasionally take over (Vol 8, Ch. 3):

- **Fragmentation / periphery risk:** because the ECB governs 20 economies, stress in weaker members shows up as a widening spread between their bond yields and Germany's (e.g. the **Italian BTP–German Bund spread**). A widening spread signals Eurozone stress and **pressures the euro** independently of the dollar. The early-2010s European debt crisis is the textbook case.
- **Energy:** the Eurozone is a major **energy importer**, so energy-price shocks hurt it. The **2022 gas crisis** (post-Ukraine invasion) was a key reason EUR/USD fell below parity.
- **Politics:** elections, EU cohesion concerns, and sovereign-debt worries can weigh on the euro.

> 🔑 Know which regime you're in. In *normal* times, trade EUR/USD as a Fed-vs-ECB-and-dollar story. In times of *European stress* (widening periphery spreads, energy shocks, political crises), the **euro's own risks** can dominate and drive the pair down regardless of the dollar. Watch the BTP–Bund spread as a euro-stress gauge.

---

## 2.8 Driver #7: positioning & expectations (what's priced in)

Finally, the meta-driver from Volume 6, Chapter 4: EUR/USD moves on the **surprise relative to expectations**, not the raw news, and extreme **positioning** creates reversal risk:

- A "good" Eurozone number that's *worse than expected* can sink the euro; a Fed decision fully priced can see "buy the rumor, sell the fact" reversals.
- When speculative positioning is extremely one-sided (e.g. everyone short EUR/USD), the pair is vulnerable to sharp counter-moves on any surprise.

> 🔑 Before any EUR/USD trade around data, ask: **"What's the consensus, and what's already priced into the pair?"** (Vol 6, Ch. 4). The move comes from the gap between reality and expectation — and crowded positioning makes the pair fragile to surprises.
