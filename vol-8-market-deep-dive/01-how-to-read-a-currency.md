Before the individual profiles, you need a **repeatable framework** for understanding *any* currency or pair — so that when a new instrument crosses your screen, you can profile it yourself. Every profile in this volume follows this exact structure; learn it once and you can read the whole market.

---

## 1.1 The seven dimensions of a currency profile

To truly *know* a currency, answer these seven questions. Together they form its complete personality:

```
1. ROLE          What is it? (reserve / European major / safe haven / commodity dollar)
2. CENTRAL BANK  Who sets its rates, and what's their stance? (Vol 6, Ch. 2)
3. DRIVERS       What moves it? (rates, data, commodities, risk sentiment, politics)
4. PERSONALITY   How does it behave? (volatile or calm; trending or ranging; orderly or wild)
5. SESSION       When is it most active? (which trading hours — Vol 1)
6. CORRELATIONS  What does it move with / against? (other pairs, commodities, yields, stocks)
7. KEY DATA      Which releases matter most for it? (its central bank + national data)
```

> 🔑 **Every currency is a bundle of these seven traits.** Once you can fill them in for an instrument, you understand *why* it moves, *when* to watch it, and *how* it tends to behave — the foundation of trading it well. The rest of this volume fills in these seven dimensions for each major.

---

## 1.2 Role — the single most useful classifier

A currency's **role** (from the README) predicts most of its behavior, especially in risk-on vs risk-off (Vol 6, Ch. 5):

| Role | Members | Behavior in RISK-ON | Behavior in RISK-OFF |
|------|---------|---------------------|----------------------|
| **Reserve / center** | USD | Mixed (depends on US growth — the dollar smile) | Strengthens (haven) |
| **European majors** | EUR, GBP | Tend to firm (esp. GBP, semi risk-on) | Soften |
| **Safe havens** | USD, JPY, CHF | Weaken (money leaves safety) | **Strengthen** (money flees to safety) |
| **Commodity dollars** | AUD, NZD, CAD | **Strengthen** (yield/growth seeking) | Weaken (risk dumped) |

> 🔑 Knowing whether a currency is a **haven** (bid in fear) or a **commodity/risk currency** (bid in optimism) instantly tells you how it'll react to the risk regime — often the dominant force on any given day. This single classifier does a lot of work.

## 1.3 Pairs combine two profiles

A currency *pair* is a **relationship** (Vol 6, Ch. 1) — so a pair's behavior is the *interaction* of two currency profiles:

- **Risk currency vs safe haven** (e.g. AUD/JPY) → a pure **risk-sentiment barometer**: rips up in risk-on, dumps in risk-off.
- **Two havens** (e.g. USD/CHF, USD/JPY) → driven more by the *rate differential* and relative haven demand than by risk direction alone.
- **Two correlated economies** (e.g. EUR/GBP, EUR/CHF) → often **range-bound**, driven by the *relative* policy/data between two similar regions.
- **Reserve vs commodity** (e.g. USD/CAD) → a tug-of-war between the dollar's drivers and the commodity's (oil).

> 🔑 To read a pair, read **both** currencies and ask what their interaction produces. A pair's personality is the *combination* — which is why AUD/JPY whipsaws on risk sentiment while EUR/GBP grinds in a range. Always profile both sides.

## 1.4 Personality — volatility and behavior

Currencies differ enormously in *how* they move, which determines position sizing (wider stops for wilder pairs — Vol 1/3) and which suit your style:

```
CALM / ORDERLY (tighter ranges, respects technicals, lower volatility):
   EUR/USD, EUR/GBP, USD/CHF — good for beginners; cleaner structure

VOLATILE / WILD (large ranges, fast moves, less forgiving):
   GBP/JPY, GBP/USD, and exotics — bigger opportunity AND bigger risk

YIELD/RISK-DRIVEN (move on macro drivers more than chart noise):
   USD/JPY (US yields), AUD/JPY (risk sentiment)
```

> 🔑 Match the pair's personality to **your style, risk tolerance, and skill** (Vol 7, Ch. 2). Beginners are usually best served by calm, liquid, orderly pairs (EUR/USD) and should treat wild pairs (GBP/JPY) with extra caution and smaller size. Volatility is opportunity *and* danger.

---

## 1.5 Liquidity, spreads & session timing

From Volume 1, every profile notes liquidity and the best session — because *when* you trade a pair matters as much as *which* you trade:

- **Major pairs** (with USD) are the most liquid, tightest-spread, and best-behaved.
- **A pair is most active when its currencies' home sessions are open** — EUR/USD in the London–NY overlap; AUD/JPY in the Asian session; etc.
- **Trade a pair during its active hours** for tighter spreads, cleaner moves, and better fills; avoid its dead hours (thin, erratic, wide spreads).

## 1.6 How to use the profiles in this volume

Each chapter profiles a currency/pair across the seven dimensions. Put them to work:

```
1. PICK YOUR FOCUS (Vol 7): choose 1–3 pairs whose personality fits your style & schedule.
2. STUDY their profiles deeply — drivers, session, correlations, key data.
3. BUILD your macro scorecards (Vol 6) using each currency's specific drivers.
4. WATCH the right things: each pair's key data, correlated markets, and session.
5. TRADE with the personality in mind: size and target appropriately for its volatility.
6. RE-CHECK against the current regime — profiles are tendencies, not guarantees.
```

> 🔑 The goal isn't to memorize every pair — it's to **deeply know the few you trade** (Vol 6, Ch. 7; Vol 7, Ch. 3). Use these profiles to choose your focus instruments and then master them: their drivers, their rhythm, their correlations, their tells. Depth in a few pairs beats shallow familiarity with all of them.
