# Chapter 4 — Support, Resistance & Zones

[← Market Structure](03-market-structure.md) | [Volume 3 Home](README.md) | [Next: Trendlines & Patterns →](05-patterns.md)

---

Structure tells you the *direction*; support and resistance (S/R) tell you the *where* — the specific prices at which the market has repeatedly made decisions. These levels are where your best trades are planned, your stops are placed, and your targets are set. **Almost every good technical trade is, at its core, a reaction at a level.**

---

## 4.1 The core idea

- **Support** = a price *floor* where falling prices have repeatedly found enough buying to bounce. Demand overwhelms supply there.
- **Resistance** = a price *ceiling* where rising prices have repeatedly stalled and reversed. Supply overwhelms demand there.

```
RESISTANCE  ─ ─ ●─ ─ ─ ─ ●─ ─ ─ ─ ●─ ─   ← price keeps getting rejected here
                \        / \      /
                 \      /   \    /
SUPPORT     ─ ─ ─ ●─ ─ ● ─ ─ ●─ ─ ─ ─    ← price keeps bouncing here
```

Why do they work? **Memory and orders.** Traders remember these prices; buyers who missed the bounce wait there, sellers who got trapped look to exit there, and resting orders cluster around them — creating a self-fulfilling reaction.

---

## 4.2 Types of support & resistance

### Horizontal S/R (the foundation)
Flat levels drawn across prior swing highs/lows where price clearly reacted. The more **touches** and the more **recent** and **higher-timeframe**, the stronger.

### Dynamic S/R (moving)
Levels that *move* with price:
- **Moving averages** (e.g. 50/200 EMA) often act as support/resistance in trends — price pulls back to a rising MA and bounces (Chapter 6).
- **Trendlines & channels** are diagonal dynamic S/R (Chapter 5).

### Psychological / round-number levels
Round numbers (**1.1000, 1.2000, 150.00**) act as S/R because humans cluster orders at "neat" prices. The "00" and "50" levels especially. Expect reactions — and frequent false breaks — around them.

### Prior-day / week / session levels & opens
Yesterday's high/low, the weekly open, session highs/lows — institutional reference points that frequently act as S/R intraday.

---

## 4.3 Polarity — the role-reversal principle ⭐

The most useful S/R concept: **when a level breaks, it flips its role.**
- **Broken resistance becomes support.** Once price closes above a ceiling and returns to it, that old ceiling often becomes a floor.
- **Broken support becomes resistance.** A floor that breaks becomes a future ceiling.

```
        ┌─── price breaks above resistance...
        │
RES ────●────────────●───  ← old resistance...
                      │
                      └──→ ...now acts as SUPPORT on the retest ("polarity flip")
```

This is gold because it gives you a **high-quality entry on the retest**: instead of chasing a breakout, you wait for price to come back to the broken level and enter as it holds — tight stop just beyond it, great risk/reward.

---

## 4.4 From lines to ZONES — the crucial upgrade

Beginners draw S/R as a *single precise line* and get frustrated when price overshoots it. **Stop thinking lines; think zones.**

- Price reacts to **areas**, not exact pixels. Draw S/R as a **band** covering the cluster of wicks/bodies where reactions happened.
- Zones account for **noise, spread, and liquidity grabs** (Chapter 3) — price may stab through the line but respect the zone.
- Use **candle bodies** for the core of the zone and **wicks** for its outer edge.

> 🔑 A zone gives you a sensible place to put a stop (beyond the *zone*, not the line) and patience to let price react within it instead of bailing on the first poke.

---

## 4.5 Supply & demand zones

A close cousin of S/R, focused on **where institutional orders originated**:

- A **demand zone** is the base/consolidation a strong *up*-move launched from — unfilled buy orders may remain there. It acts as support.
- A **supply zone** is the base a strong *down*-move launched from — resting sell orders. It acts as resistance.
- **How to spot one:** find a sharp, impulsive move ("departure") and mark the small consolidation it exploded *from*. That origin is the zone.

The key difference from plain S/R: supply/demand zones can be **fresh** (untested since formation) — and fresh zones, especially those that *caused* a strong move, often give the cleanest reactions on their **first** retest.

---

## 4.6 How to draw good levels (a repeatable method)

```
1. Start on a HIGHER timeframe (D1/H4). Major levels there matter most.
2. Mark prices where price CLEARLY reversed or paused MORE THAN ONCE.
3. Prefer levels with: multiple touches, recent relevance, and a strong reaction.
4. Draw them as ZONES (bands), using bodies for the core, wicks for the edges.
5. Note broken levels — they become future polarity (flipped) S/R.
6. Drop to your trading timeframe and refine entries AT these HTF levels.
7. Keep it minimal — the 3–6 levels that matter, not 30. A cluttered chart is useless.
```

**Strength checklist — a level is stronger when it has:**
- More touches/reactions ✔
- A stronger, more impulsive reaction away from it ✔
- Higher-timeframe origin ✔
- Confluence with other tools (round number, Fib, MA, trendline) ✔ (Chapter 7)

---

## 4.7 Trading with S/R — the three core plays

1. **The bounce (range/with-trend):** buy at support / sell at resistance, *with a confirming candle signal* (Chapter 2), stop beyond the zone, target the opposite level. Best when the level is strong and untested-recently.
2. **The breakout:** trade *through* a level when price closes decisively beyond it (Chapter 5). Risk: false breaks — require a close, and consider waiting for the retest.
3. **The retest (polarity):** the highest-quality version — wait for a broken level to be retested as flipped S/R and enter on the hold. Tight stop, clean invalidation, strong R/R.

> ⚠️ **Don't blindly trade every level.** A level is a *location of interest*, not an automatic trade. You still need trend alignment, a trigger, and good risk/reward. Levels tell you **where** to pay attention — the rest of this volume tells you **whether** and **how** to act.

---

## 4.8 Chapter summary

```
• Support = floor (demand); Resistance = ceiling (supply).
• Types: horizontal, dynamic (MAs/trendlines), psychological round numbers, session levels.
• POLARITY: broken resistance → support, broken support → resistance (great retest entries).
• Think ZONES, not lines — price reacts to areas; place stops beyond the zone.
• Supply/demand zones = the origin of strong impulsive moves; fresh ones react cleanest.
• Draw levels top-down, mark only the few that matter, favor multi-touch HTF zones.
• Three plays: the bounce, the breakout, the retest — always with trend + trigger + risk.
```
