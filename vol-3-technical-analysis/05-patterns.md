# Chapter 5 — Trendlines, Channels & Chart Patterns

---

Chart patterns are recurring *shapes* that price draws as the buyer/seller balance shifts. They're really just **structure and S/R in recognizable packages** — which is why this chapter comes *after* you've learned both. Used with discipline, patterns give you a defined entry, a logical stop, and a measured target. Used as magic shapes to spot everywhere, they're a trap.

> ⚠️ **Patterns are probabilistic and subjective.** Two traders can draw the same chart differently, and plenty of "textbook" patterns fail. They are a framework for organizing a trade, **not** a guarantee. Trade them *with* trend, structure, levels, and risk control — never on the pretty shape alone.

---

## 5.1 Trendlines

A **trendline** connects swing points to visualize a trend's slope and act as **dynamic, diagonal S/R**.
- **Uptrend line:** connect rising **swing lows**. Price pulling back to it can offer with-trend longs.
- **Downtrend line:** connect falling **swing highs**. Pullbacks to it can offer with-trend shorts.

**Rules for valid trendlines:**
- Needs **at least two points** to draw, and a **third touch** to confirm validity.
- Connect **wicks or bodies consistently** — don't cherry-pick to force a line.
- The more touches and the shallower the angle, the more reliable. A near-vertical line is unsustainable.
- A **decisive close through** a trendline is a possible trend-change/continuation signal — but beware false breaks (require a close; watch for the retest).

> 🔑 Trendlines are *guides*, not laws. If you have to twist and re-draw a line to keep price "respecting" it, the trend has probably changed. Let the chart tell you, don't impose your line on it.

---

## 5.2 Channels

A **channel** is two parallel trendlines containing price — a visual of a trending range.
- **Ascending / descending channels:** trade *bounces* within them (buy the lower rail in an up-channel, sell the upper rail in a down-channel) and watch for **breakouts** of either rail.
- **Horizontal channel = a range/rectangle** (Section 5.6).
- The **mid-line** often acts as minor S/R.

Channels give clean, repeatable swing trades *and* a defined breakout point. The breakout of a channel against its slope can signal a trend change.

## 5.3 Reversal patterns

These form at the **end of a trend** and signal a potential turn. Highest odds when they appear at a major **HTF level** after an extended move.

### Head & Shoulders (and Inverse) ⭐
The classic reversal.
- **Head & Shoulders (top):** a peak (left shoulder), a higher peak (head), a lower peak (right shoulder), all sitting on a **neckline** (support). A close **below the neckline** confirms the reversal.
- **Inverse H&S (bottom):** the mirror image at a low — confirms on a close **above** the neckline.

```
        Head
        /\
   LS  /  \  RS          Left & right shoulders roughly even,
   /\ /    \ /\          head higher; neckline connects the lows.
  /  V      V  \
 ───────────────── neckline  ← break & close below = bearish confirmation
```
**Target (measured move):** the height from head to neckline, projected from the breakout point.

### Double Top / Double Bottom ⭐
Price tests a level **twice** and fails — two peaks at resistance (double top, "M") or two troughs at support (double bottom, "W"). Confirms when price breaks the level between the two turns (the "neckline"). **Triple tops/bottoms** are the three-touch version.

### Rounding Top / Bottom (saucer)
A slow, curved transition of control — gradual rather than sharp. Signals a gentle trend change over many candles.

## 5.4 Continuation patterns

These form *within* a trend as it **pauses to consolidate**, then typically resolve in the trend's direction.

### Flags & Pennants ⭐
After a strong impulsive move (the "flagpole"), price consolidates in a small **counter-sloping channel (flag)** or **small symmetrical triangle (pennant)**, then breaks out to continue the trend.
- High-probability *with-trend* setups.
- **Target:** often the flagpole's length projected from the breakout.

### Triangles
- **Ascending triangle:** flat resistance on top, **rising** support below → buyers getting aggressive → usually breaks **up** (bullish bias).
- **Descending triangle:** flat support below, **falling** resistance above → usually breaks **down** (bearish bias).
- **Symmetrical triangle:** converging highs and lows → **neutral**; trade the breakout direction. Often continues the prior trend.

```
Ascending triangle:                 Descending triangle:
RES ──────────────  (flat)              \
        /    /                            \   (falling)
       /    /  (rising support)            \
      /    /                          ──────────────  RES flat support
   → tends to break UP                → tends to break DOWN
```

### Rectangles (ranges)
Price bounces between horizontal support and resistance. Trade the edges (range), or trade the **breakout** when one side gives way. (This is just a range — see Chapter 4.)

### Cup & Handle
A rounded bottom ("cup") followed by a small pullback ("handle"), then a breakout higher. A bullish continuation pattern.

---

## 5.5 Wedges

Both trendlines slope the **same** direction but **converge**:
- **Rising wedge:** both lines rise, converging → typically **bearish** (especially after an uptrend — buyers exhausting).
- **Falling wedge:** both lines fall, converging → typically **bullish** (sellers exhausting).

Wedges can be reversal **or** continuation depending on context — the converging momentum is the tell: the trend is running out of steam in the wedge's direction.

---

## 5.6 The measured move — projecting targets

Most patterns come with a built-in **target** via their own size:
- **Triangles/rectangles:** project the **height** of the pattern from the breakout point.
- **Flags/pennants:** project the **flagpole** length from the breakout.
- **H&S / double tops:** project the pattern's **height** (head-to-neckline / level-to-neckline) from the break.

This gives an **objective, pre-defined target** so you can check the risk/reward *before* entering — and only take the trade if reward ≥ ~2× risk.

## 5.7 Trading patterns without getting trapped

```
1. CONTEXT     □ Does the pattern fit structure & the HTF trend?
                 (continuation patterns with-trend = highest odds)
2. LOCATION    □ Is it forming at/around a meaningful level?
3. CONFIRMATION□ Wait for a CLOSE beyond the pattern boundary (not a wick poke).
                 Consider entering on the RETEST of the broken boundary.
4. STOP        □ Beyond the opposite side of the pattern / the invalidation point.
5. TARGET      □ Measured move and/or next major level. Require R/R ≥ ~2:1.
6. INVALIDATE  □ Know what price action says "this pattern failed" — and honor it.
```

### The biggest pattern mistakes
- ❌ **Front-running** the breakout (entering before a confirming close) → caught by false breaks.
- ❌ **Forcing patterns** that aren't really there (seeing H&S in random noise).
- ❌ **Ignoring the trend** — fighting a strong trend on a small reversal pattern.
- ❌ **No measured target** — entering with no idea of reward vs risk.
- ❌ **Trading tiny patterns on low timeframes** — mostly noise.

> 🔑 Patterns are most powerful as **confluence** (Chapter 7): a bull flag *at* a demand zone *in* an uptrend *with* a bullish trigger candle is a far better trade than any one of those alone. The shape is the packaging; trend + level + trigger + risk is the substance.
