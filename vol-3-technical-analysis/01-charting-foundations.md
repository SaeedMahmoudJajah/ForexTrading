Before patterns and indicators, you must be fluent in the **raw language of the chart**: what each chart type shows, how timeframes change the story, and how to *read price itself*. Master this and everything later clicks into place.

---

## 1.1 What a chart actually is

A price chart is a **map of the battle between buyers and sellers over time.** The horizontal axis is time; the vertical axis is price. Every wiggle is the net result of supply meeting demand. Technical analysis is simply the disciplined reading of that map to find spots where the odds favor one side.

Three assumptions underpin all TA:
1. **Price discounts everything** — all known information is already reflected in the price.
2. **Price moves in trends** — and a trend in motion is more likely to continue than reverse, until it doesn't.
3. **History rhymes** — because human behavior (fear/greed) repeats, patterns tend to recur.

> 🔑 These are *tendencies*, not laws. TA gives you an **edge over many trades**, never certainty on any one.

## 1.2 Chart types — and which to use

### Line chart
Connects closing prices only. Clean and uncluttered — great for seeing the **big-picture trend** at a glance, but it hides the intra-period action (highs, lows, opens). Use it to step back, not to time entries.

### Bar chart (OHLC)
Each bar shows **Open, High, Low, Close**. The left tick is the open, the right tick is the close, the vertical line spans the high–low range. Information-rich but visually harder to parse than candles.

### Candlestick chart ⭐ (the standard)
Same OHLC data as a bar, but drawn as a **body + wicks** that makes momentum and rejection jump out visually. This is what virtually all traders use and what the rest of this volume assumes.

```
  Bullish candle          Bearish candle
        │  ← high               │  ← high
       ┌┴┐ ← close             ┌┴┐ ← open
       │ │                     │ │ ← body
       │ │ ← body              │ │
       └┬┘ ← open              └┬┘ ← close
        │  ← low                │  ← low
   close > open            close < open
   (often green/white)     (often red/black)
```

### Heikin Ashi
A *modified* candlestick that averages price to **smooth out noise** and make trends visually obvious (long runs of same-color candles). Trade-off: the displayed open/close are averaged, **not** the real prices — never read exact entry levels off it. Good for trend context, bad for precise execution.

### Renko / Range / Point-and-figure
Charts that strip out **time** and plot only meaningful price movement (e.g. a new brick every X pips). They filter noise and highlight trends/levels, at the cost of timing information. Useful as a secondary lens, not a primary one for beginners.

> 🔑 **Default: candlesticks** for analysis and execution; a **line or Heikin Ashi** to sanity-check the bigger trend.

## 1.3 Timeframes — the same chart tells different stories

The timeframe is the duration each candle represents. The *identical* market looks bullish on one timeframe and bearish on another — which is why pros always look at more than one (Chapter 8).

| Group | Timeframes | Typical use |
|-------|-----------|-------------|
| **Higher (HTF)** | Weekly (W1), Daily (D1), 4-hour (H4) | Overall trend, major levels, *bias* |
| **Intermediate** | 1-hour (H1), 30-min (M30) | Setup development, structure |
| **Lower (LTF)** | 15-min (M15), 5-min (M5), 1-min (M1) | Entry timing, fine-tuning stops |

- **Higher timeframes are more reliable** — each candle holds more data, so levels and trends carry more weight. A daily support level matters far more than a 5-minute one.
- **Lower timeframes are noisier** — more signals, more false moves, more emotional whipsaw.
- Your **trading style** sets your timeframes: swing traders live on H4/D1; day traders on M15/H1; scalpers on M1/M5. (See Volume 1, Strategies.)

> 🔑 **The "rule of ~4–6×":** pair a higher timeframe for *bias* with a lower one ~4–6× faster for *entries* (e.g. D1 + H1, or H4 + M15). Don't jump between wildly disconnected timeframes.

## 1.4 How to actually read price (before any indicator)

Open a clean candlestick chart — no indicators — and ask, in order:

1. **Which way is it trending?** Up, down, or sideways? (Higher highs/lows vs lower highs/lows — Chapter 3.)
2. **Where are the obvious levels?** The prices where price has clearly reversed or paused before (Chapter 4).
3. **What is momentum doing right now?** Big strong candles in one direction = momentum. Small indecisive candles or long wicks = hesitation/rejection (Chapter 2).
4. **Where is price *relative* to structure and levels?** At support? Mid-range? Pushing into resistance? This decides whether a setup is high- or low-quality.

> 🔑 **Naked-chart skill comes first.** If you can't read trend, levels, and momentum on a bare candlestick chart, no indicator will save you. Indicators (Chapter 6) *confirm* this reading — they never replace it.

## 1.5 Reading momentum and conviction in the candles

Even before formal patterns, the *shape* of candles tells you who's in control:

- **Big bodies, small wicks** → strong conviction in the body's direction.
- **Small bodies, long wicks** → indecision or rejection; the controlling side failed to hold the extreme.
- **A series of higher-closing bullish candles** → healthy demand. **A cluster of long upper wicks at a high** → sellers defending; momentum stalling.
- **Expanding candle ranges** → rising volatility/interest. **Shrinking ranges** → consolidation; a move is often building.

This "feel" for momentum is what separates someone who *reads* a chart from someone who just looks at it.

## 1.6 Chart hygiene — set yourself up to see clearly

- **Keep it clean.** One or two indicators *maximum*. A chart buried under 8 indicators creates contradictory signals and analysis paralysis.
- **Use consistent colors** and a layout you can read instantly.
- **Mark only the levels that matter** — major swing highs/lows and clear S/R. Don't graffiti the chart.
- **Log timeframes you analyze** so your process is repeatable (the journal from Volume 1's templates).
- **Use a quality charting platform** (e.g. TradingView, or MT4/MT5) and practice on replay/demo.

## 1.7 Chapter summary

```
• A chart maps the buyer/seller battle over time; TA reads it for an EDGE, not certainty.
• Default to CANDLESTICKS; use line/Heikin Ashi to confirm the bigger trend.
• Higher timeframes = reliable bias; lower timeframes = noisy timing. Pair them ~4–6× apart.
• Read price in order: TREND → LEVELS → MOMENTUM → location of price within them.
• Candle shape (body vs wick) reveals conviction vs rejection BEFORE any indicator.
• Keep charts clean: ≤2 indicators, only meaningful levels marked.
```
