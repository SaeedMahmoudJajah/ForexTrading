# Chapter 2 — Candlestick Mastery

---

Candlesticks are the alphabet of price action. Each candle is a tiny story of who won the period; each *combination* is a sentence. This chapter makes you fluent. **But read the warning first**, because candlestick patterns are the most over-hyped tool in retail trading.

> ⚠️ **The candle is context-dependent.** The *same* pattern means almost nothing in the middle of a range and a great deal at a major support level after an extended move. **A candlestick signal is only tradeable when it appears at a meaningful location (Chapters 3–4) and ideally with confluence (Chapter 7).** Pattern-spotting in a vacuum is how beginners lose money confidently.

---

## 2.1 Anatomy of a candle (recap, deeper)

```
        │  ← Upper wick (shadow): the HIGH. Shows how far buyers pushed
       ┌┴┐    before being rejected back.
       │ │ ← Body: the range between OPEN and CLOSE. The "result" of the period.
       └┬┘
        │  ← Lower wick (shadow): the LOW. Shows how far sellers pushed
           before being rejected back.
```

- **Body = conviction.** A large body means one side dominated the close.
- **Wick = rejection.** A long wick means price went there and was *forcibly rejected* — that extreme was defended.
- **Open/Close position** tells you the *net* outcome; **High/Low** tell you the *fight* along the way.

> 🔑 Read every candle as: *"Where did they try to go, and where did they actually end up?"* The gap between the two (wick vs body) is the information.

## 2.2 Single-candle signals

| Candle | Look | Meaning |
|--------|------|---------|
| **Marubozu** | Big body, ~no wicks | Total domination by one side; strong momentum |
| **Doji** | Tiny/no body, wicks both sides | Indecision; balance — often precedes a turn at extremes |
| **Spinning top** | Small body, wicks both sides | Weak indecision; momentum fading |
| **Hammer** | Small body up top, long *lower* wick | At a *downtrend low*: sellers pushed down but buyers slammed it back → bullish reversal clue |
| **Hanging man** | Same shape as hammer | At an *uptrend high*: warning of weakening demand → bearish clue |
| **Inverted hammer** | Small body at bottom, long *upper* wick | At a low: bullish reversal clue (buyers tested higher) |
| **Shooting star** | Same shape as inverted hammer | At a high: buyers pushed up then got rejected → bearish reversal clue |

**Key insight:** Hammer vs hanging man (and inverted hammer vs shooting star) are the **same shape** — the *location* (at a low vs at a high) determines the meaning. This is the whole lesson of the chapter in miniature: **context defines the signal.**

## 2.3 The pin bar (the trader's favorite)

A **pin bar** is any candle with a small body and one long wick (a "rejection candle" — hammer/shooting star family). It's beloved because it visually screams *"price tried to go here and was violently rejected."*

```
A bullish pin bar at support:        A bearish pin bar at resistance:
        ┌─┐                                  │
        │ │ ← small body near top            │ ← long upper wick (rejection)
        │ │                                 ┌┴┐
        │ │ ← LONG lower wick               │ │ ← small body near bottom
        │ │   (rejection of lows)           └─┘
```

**What makes a pin bar tradeable:**
- The wick is **at least ~2× the body** and clearly longer than surrounding candles.
- It forms **at a key level** (support/resistance/supply-demand), not mid-range.
- It rejects **into the direction you want to trade** (long lower wick at support → long bias).
- Bonus: the wick pokes *beyond* a level and closes back inside (a "liquidity grab" / false break).

## 2.4 Two-candle patterns

### Bullish / Bearish Engulfing ⭐
A candle whose **body completely engulfs** the prior candle's body, in the opposite direction. The most reliable two-candle reversal signal.
- **Bullish engulfing:** a big up-candle swallows the prior down-candle — buyers overwhelmed sellers. Strong at a support level after a decline.
- **Bearish engulfing:** a big down-candle swallows the prior up-candle — sellers took over. Strong at resistance after a rally.

### Harami (inside candle reversal)
The opposite shape: a **small candle contained within** the prior large candle's body. Signals momentum **stalling** — the prior trend is losing steam. Weaker than engulfing; treat as a "pause/warning."

### Piercing Line / Dark Cloud Cover
- **Piercing line (bullish):** a down-candle, then an up-candle that closes **above the midpoint** of the prior body. Reversal clue at support.
- **Dark cloud cover (bearish):** an up-candle, then a down-candle closing **below the midpoint** of the prior body. Reversal clue at resistance.

### Tweezer Tops / Bottoms
Two (or more) candles with **matching highs (tops)** or **matching lows (bottoms)** — price tested the same extreme twice and failed. A double-rejection of a level.

## 2.5 Three-candle patterns

### Morning Star / Evening Star ⭐
Powerful three-candle reversals:
- **Morning star (bullish):** big down-candle → small indecisive candle (the "star," often a doji) → big up-candle. Marks a bottom: capitulation, pause, then buyers seize control.
- **Evening star (bearish):** the mirror at a top: big up-candle → small star → big down-candle.

```
Morning star (bottom reversal):
   █                       ← 1) strong bearish candle
   █
      ─   ← 2) small "star" (indecision / gap of momentum)
         █                 ← 3) strong bullish candle reclaiming ground
         █
```

### Three White Soldiers / Three Black Crows
Three consecutive strong candles in one direction (each opening within the prior body and closing higher/lower). Signals **strong, sustained momentum** — often a trend's start or powerful continuation. Beware chasing if they're already extended.

## 2.6 How to actually trade a candlestick signal

A repeatable filter so you only act on *quality* signals:

```
1. LOCATION   □ Is the candle at a meaningful level (S/R, supply/demand, structure)?
2. CONTEXT    □ Does it fit the higher-timeframe trend/bias? (with-trend = higher odds)
3. THE SIGNAL □ Is the pattern clean and clear (e.g. wick ≥2× body, real engulfing)?
4. CONFIRM    □ Optional: does the next candle / an indicator agree?
5. RISK       □ Stop goes beyond the candle's wick/level; target ≥2× the risk.

If 1–3 aren't all YES, it's not a setup. Skip it.
```

### Entry tactics
- **Aggressive:** enter on the **close** of the signal candle (better price, more risk it fails).
- **Conservative:** wait for the **next candle to confirm** the direction (or a small pullback to the signal candle), accepting a slightly worse price for higher reliability.
- **Stop placement:** just beyond the **rejection wick** or the level — the point that proves the signal failed.

---

## 2.7 Candlestick traps to avoid

- ❌ **Trading patterns mid-range** with no level → coin-flip noise.
- ❌ **Counter-trend hero trades** on one candle against a strong higher-timeframe trend.
- ❌ **Lower-timeframe pattern obsession** (M1/M5) — far more noise, far more false signals.
- ❌ **Ignoring the close.** A wick poking a level intrabar is not the same as a *closed* candle confirming. Wait for the close.
- ❌ **Forgetting cost.** A 3-pip "signal" eaten by spread isn't an edge.

> 🔑 **The candle is the trigger, never the thesis.** Your thesis comes from trend + structure + levels (next two chapters). The candlestick just tells you *when* the level is being respected so you can enter with tight, defined risk. Get this order right and candlesticks become genuinely powerful; get it backwards and they're a trap.
