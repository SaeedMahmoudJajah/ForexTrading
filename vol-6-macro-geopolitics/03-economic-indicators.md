# Chapter 3 — The Economic Indicators That Move Currencies

[← Central Banks & Interest Rates](02-central-banks.md) | [Volume 6 Home](README.md) | [Next: The Expectations Game →](04-expectations-game.md)

---

Economic indicators are the **data that tells the market how an economy is doing** — and, crucially, **what the central bank is likely to do next** (Chapter 2). There are dozens of releases; you don't need them all. This chapter tiers them by market impact and explains *how* each affects a currency, so you can focus on what moves money and ignore the noise.

> 🔑 **The golden rule for reading any indicator:** always ask *"what does this mean for the central bank's next move, relative to expectations and to the other economy?"* An indicator only matters to FX through its effect on **rate expectations** (or, in crises, risk sentiment). That single question turns raw data into a trading signal.

---

## 3.1 How to think about indicators

Three useful classifications:

- **Leading** indicators *predict* future activity (PMIs, building permits, consumer expectations, the yield curve). Markets love them — they hint at what's coming.
- **Coincident** indicators describe the *present* (GDP, employment, industrial production).
- **Lagging** indicators confirm the *past* (the unemployment rate, CPI to a degree).

And the impact tiering you actually trade by:

```
TIER 1 (high impact — can move markets hard):
   Central-bank rate decisions & guidance · Inflation (CPI/PCE) · Jobs report (NFP) ·
   GDP · PMIs (ISM) · central-bank speeches
TIER 2 (moderate):
   Retail sales · employment sub-data (claims, JOLTS, ADP) · trade balance ·
   consumer confidence · PPI · durable goods · industrial production
TIER 3 (usually minor, context only):
   Housing details · second-tier surveys · revisions of old data
```

---

## 3.2 Inflation — the central bank's obsession ⭐

Because central banks are mandated to keep prices stable, **inflation data is among the most market-moving of all** — it directly drives rate expectations (Chapter 2).

| Indicator | What it is | Why it matters |
|-----------|-----------|----------------|
| **CPI (Consumer Price Index)** ⭐ | The headline measure of consumer price inflation | The big one. Hot CPI → hike expectations rise → **currency up**; cooling CPI → cut expectations → **currency down** |
| **Core CPI** | CPI excluding volatile food & energy | The *trend* signal central banks weigh most |
| **PCE (Personal Consumption Expenditures)** | The **Fed's preferred** inflation gauge (esp. Core PCE) | Watch closely for US/Fed expectations |
| **PPI (Producer Price Index)** | Inflation at the producer/wholesale level | A *leading* hint for future consumer inflation |

**The mechanism:** higher-than-expected inflation → pressure on the central bank to hike (or stay hawkish) → higher expected yields → **currency strengthens** (up to the point where inflation is seen as *out of control / behind the curve*, which can flip the effect). Cooling inflation → room to cut → **currency weakens.**

> 🔑 In an inflation-fighting regime, **CPI day is often the single most volatile day of the month** — frequently bigger than the jobs report. Know when it's released and respect it (Chapter 4 & 8).

## 3.3 Employment — the health of the economy ⭐

Strong employment signals a healthy economy that can withstand (and may require) tighter policy. The US jobs report is the most-watched employment data on earth.

| Indicator | What it is | Currency effect (broadly) |
|-----------|-----------|---------------------------|
| **Non-Farm Payrolls (NFP)** ⭐ | US jobs added/lost, 1st Friday monthly | Strong → hawkish-leaning → **USD up**; weak → **USD down**. Huge, fast volatility |
| **Unemployment rate** | % of workforce jobless | Lower = stronger economy (lagging indicator) |
| **Average hourly earnings (wages)** ⭐ | Wage growth | Critical for *inflation* — rising wages → inflation pressure → hawkish. Often the most important line in the report |
| **Weekly jobless claims** | New unemployment filings | High-frequency, leading read on the labor market |
| **JOLTS / ADP** | Job openings / private payrolls | Secondary labor reads markets use to anticipate NFP |

**The nuance:** markets read jobs data *through the inflation/policy lens.* In an inflation-fighting regime, "too strong" jobs/wages can be read as **hawkish** (more hikes needed) and lift the currency; in a slowdown regime, strong jobs simply mean resilience. **Wages** are often the most important number because they feed directly into inflation.

> 🔑 NFP is famous for violent, two-way spikes and slippage. Many traders **stand aside** through the release and trade the *clearer trend* afterward (Chapter 8). Don't get chopped up trying to trade the first second.

## 3.4 Growth & activity — the economy's momentum

| Indicator | What it is | Why it matters |
|-----------|-----------|----------------|
| **GDP (Gross Domestic Product)** ⭐ | Total economic output, quarterly | The broadest health gauge. Stronger-than-expected growth → currency-supportive |
| **PMIs (Purchasing Managers' Indexes)** ⭐ | Monthly business surveys (manufacturing & services). **ISM** in the US | **Leading** indicator. The **50 line** divides expansion (>50) from contraction (<50). A drop below 50, or a big miss, moves markets fast |
| **Retail sales** ⭐ | Consumer spending | Consumption is the bulk of developed economies → a key activity read |
| **Industrial production / durable goods** | Factory output / big-ticket orders | Cyclical activity signals |

**PMIs deserve special attention:** because they're *leading* and released early each month, they often shape expectations *before* the lagging hard data confirms it. A series of sub-50 PMIs warning of contraction can shift rate-cut expectations and weaken a currency well ahead of GDP.

---

## 3.5 Sentiment & confidence — the forward look

Surveys of how consumers and businesses *feel*, which predict future spending/investment:

- **Consumer confidence / sentiment** (e.g. Conference Board, University of Michigan in the US) — optimistic consumers spend more.
- **Business confidence** (e.g. Germany's **Ifo** and **ZEW** surveys) — leading reads on investment and the EUR.

These are **leading** and can move markets when they surprise, especially at economic turning points.

---

## 3.6 External balances — trade & capital flows

| Indicator | What it is | Currency relevance |
|-----------|-----------|---------------------|
| **Trade balance** | Exports minus imports | Persistent surpluses imply structural demand for the currency (foreigners buying exports must buy the currency); deficits the reverse |
| **Current account** | Broader external balance (trade + income flows) | A structural, slower-moving driver of currency value |

These matter more for **long-term, structural** currency trends than for day-to-day moves, but they frame which currencies have underlying support or vulnerability.

## 3.7 The interpretation framework — turning data into a decision

For *every* release, run this:

```
1. ACTUAL vs FORECAST: did it beat or miss the CONSENSUS expectation? (Ch. 4)
   → It's the SURPRISE that moves price, not the raw number.
2. POLICY IMPLICATION: does this push the central bank more HAWKISH or DOVISH?
   → Hotter inflation / stronger jobs+wages / better growth  → hawkish → currency up
   → Cooler inflation / weaker jobs / contraction (PMI<50)   → dovish  → currency down
3. RELATIVE: how does this compare to the OTHER currency's data trend? (Ch. 1)
4. REGIME CHECK: which lens is the market using right now — inflation-fighting
   (strong data = hawkish = currency up) or growth-fear (weak data = risk-off)?
5. PRICED IN?: was this already expected and reflected in the price? (Ch. 4)
```

> ⚠️ **The same number means different things in different regimes.** In 2022's inflation fight, strong US jobs lifted the dollar (more Fed hikes). In a recession scare, strong jobs might lift risk appetite and *weaken* the dollar as a haven. **Always identify the regime (Ch. 5) before interpreting the data.**

## 3.8 What you actually need to track

You don't monitor 50 indicators. For each currency you trade, follow the **Tier 1 set** and the trend in a few Tier 2 reads:

```
PER CURRENCY, TRACK THE TREND IN:
  • Inflation (CPI / core / the bank's preferred gauge)   → policy direction
  • Jobs (headline + WAGES)                                → policy direction
  • Growth (GDP + PMIs)                                    → momentum & leading signal
  • + the central-bank decisions/speeches that interpret all of it (Ch. 2)

You're building a simple verdict per currency:
  "Is this economy's data trending in a way that makes its central bank MORE hawkish
   or MORE dovish than the market expects — relative to the other currency?"
```

That verdict, maintained over time, is your fundamental bias. The next chapter sharpens it with the most important subtlety of all: expectations.

## 3.9 Chapter summary

```
• Indicators matter to FX only through their effect on RATE EXPECTATIONS (or risk sentiment).
  Always ask: "what does this mean for the central bank's next move, vs expectations?"
• Tier 1 movers: rate decisions, INFLATION (CPI/PCE), JOBS (NFP + wages), GDP, PMIs, CB speeches.
• Inflation is the central bank's obsession → CPI is often the most volatile day of the month.
• Jobs read through the inflation lens; WAGES are frequently the key line. NFP = violent spikes.
• PMIs are LEADING (50 = expansion/contraction line) and shape expectations early.
• Trade/current-account balances drive STRUCTURAL, longer-term currency trends.
• Interpret every release: actual vs forecast (the SURPRISE) → hawkish/dovish implication →
  vs the other currency → check the REGIME → is it already priced in?
• The SAME data means different things in different regimes — identify the regime first.
```
