# Chapter 2 — Central Banks & Interest Rates

[← The Macro Mindset](01-macro-mindset.md) | [Volume 6 Home](README.md) | [Next: Economic Indicators →](03-economic-indicators.md)

---

If you learn one chapter in this volume cold, make it this one. **Central banks and the interest rates they set are the single most powerful force in the currency market.** Nearly every other fundamental — inflation, jobs, growth — matters *primarily because of what it implies for central-bank policy.* Understand the central banks and you understand the engine that drives FX.

---

## 2.1 Why interest rates dominate currencies

An interest rate is, simply, **the return you earn for holding a currency.** Global capital chases the best risk-adjusted return, so:

- **Higher (or rising) rates** attract foreign capital seeking that yield → demand for the currency → it **strengthens.**
- **Lower (or falling) rates** make a currency less rewarding to hold → capital leaves → it **weakens.**

This is the **return** channel from Chapter 1, in its purest form. When the Fed raises US rates, dollars earn more, global money flows into dollar assets, and the dollar tends to rise — all else equal.

> 🔑 **Currencies trade on interest-rate *differentials and their expected path*** — the difference between what two currencies pay, and where that difference is *heading.* EUR/USD is, at its core, a bet on "ECB policy path vs Fed policy path."

---

## 2.2 The crucial nuance: it's EXPECTATIONS, not the current rate

This is where beginners go wrong. Markets are forward-looking — **today's price already reflects the current rate and the *expected* path.** What moves the currency is a **change in those expectations.**

- A rate *hike* that everyone fully expected can cause the currency to **fall** ("priced in" — and traders take profit; Chapter 4).
- A *hold* paired with surprisingly **hawkish** language (hinting at future hikes) can cause the currency to **rally** sharply.
- The currency is driven by shifts in *"where will rates be in 6–18 months?"* more than by today's decision.

> 🔑 **You're not trading the rate — you're trading the market's evolving expectation of the *future rate path.*** This is why central-bank *tone and guidance* often matter more than the decision itself.

---

## 2.3 Hawkish vs dovish — the vocabulary that moves billions

Central-bank communication is decoded along one axis:

| Stance | Means | Generally currency... |
|--------|-------|------------------------|
| **Hawkish** 🦅 | Leaning to *tighten*: raise rates / reduce stimulus (usually to fight inflation) | **Positive** (higher future yield) |
| **Dovish** 🕊️ | Leaning to *ease*: cut rates / add stimulus (usually to support growth/jobs) | **Negative** (lower future yield) |

The market scrutinizes every word for shifts on this axis — in the statement, the press conference, projections, and individual policymakers' speeches. A central bank turning **more hawkish than expected** is one of the most reliable bullish catalysts for a currency (and vice versa).

> 🔑 Learn to read the *direction of the shift* relative to expectations. "Dovish hike" (raising rates but signaling it's the last one) can *weaken* a currency; "hawkish hold" (no change but threatening hikes) can *strengthen* it. The label on the action matters less than the **signal about the path.**

---

## 2.4 Real interest rates — the pro's refinement

Sophisticated macro traders watch **real** rates, not just nominal ones:

```
Real interest rate  ≈  Nominal interest rate  −  Inflation
```

A 5% nominal rate with 6% inflation is a **negative real return** — your money loses purchasing power. Currencies are often best supported when **real** yields are high and rising (the central bank is ahead of inflation), and pressured when high inflation outpaces rates (real yields negative).

This is why the **inflation-vs-rates relationship** is central (Chapter 3): markets constantly judge whether a central bank is *ahead of* or *behind* inflation. "Behind the curve" (rates too low for the inflation) can eventually weaken a currency despite nominal hikes.

---

## 2.5 The carry trade — getting paid to hold

The rate differential creates the **carry trade**, a core macro strategy:

- **Borrow/sell a low-yielding currency** (historically the Japanese yen, with near-zero rates for decades) and **buy a high-yielding currency** (e.g. at times AUD, NZD, USD, or an emerging-market currency).
- You earn the **interest-rate difference** (positive swap/rollover — Vol 1) just for holding, *plus* any favorable price move.

⚠️ **The danger:** carry trades work in **calm, risk-on** conditions but **unwind violently in risk-off** episodes. When fear hits, traders dump the high-yielder and rush back into the funding currency (yen), causing sharp, fast reversals. This is a major reason the **yen strengthens during crises** — global carry trades unwind. (Links to risk sentiment, Chapter 5.)

> 🔑 Carry explains a lot of "calm-market" currency drift (slow grind toward high-yielders) *and* a lot of sudden crisis spikes (the violent unwind). Knowing who's "funding" and who's the "high-yielder" tells you which currencies are fragile in a risk-off shock.

---

## 2.6 Beyond rates: QE, QT & the balance sheet

Central banks have a second, powerful lever — **the money supply / their balance sheet:**

- **Quantitative Easing (QE):** the central bank *creates money* to buy bonds, flooding the system with liquidity, suppressing yields, and generally **weakening** the currency (more of it, lower yields). Used in crises/recessions.
- **Quantitative Tightening (QT):** the reverse — shrinking the balance sheet, draining liquidity — generally **supportive** of the currency.
- **Liquidity is a master variable.** Druckenmiller's emphasis (Vol 2) — *"focus on the movement of liquidity"* — is about exactly this: expanding global liquidity (QE) inflates risk assets and can weaken havens; contracting liquidity (QT) does the opposite.

Forward guidance, yield-curve control (the BoJ's historical tool), and emergency facilities are further instruments — all read through the same hawkish/dovish, liquidity-expanding/contracting lens.

---

## 2.7 The major central banks — who to watch

Each major currency has a central bank you must track. Know their meeting schedules (most meet roughly every 6 weeks) and mandates.

| Bank | Currency | Notes / mandate |
|------|----------|-----------------|
| **Federal Reserve (Fed/FOMC)** 🇺🇸 | USD | The most important in the world — USD is the global reserve currency, so Fed policy moves *everything*. Dual mandate: price stability + maximum employment. Publishes the **"dot plot"** of rate projections. |
| **European Central Bank (ECB)** 🇪🇺 | EUR | Sets policy for the whole Eurozone; primary mandate is price stability. Watch for divergence between member economies. |
| **Bank of England (BoE/MPC)** 🇬🇧 | GBP | Votes are published (the MPC split signals the path). |
| **Bank of Japan (BoJ)** 🇯🇵 | JPY | Famously ultra-loose for decades (near-zero rates, QE, yield-curve control); shifts are huge market events. JPY is a haven & key carry funding currency. |
| **Swiss National Bank (SNB)** 🇨🇭 | CHF | CHF is a safe haven; the SNB has a history of intervention (e.g. the 2015 removal of the EUR/CHF floor). |
| **Bank of Canada (BoC)** 🇨🇦 | CAD | CAD is oil-sensitive (Chapter 6). |
| **Reserve Bank of Australia (RBA)** 🇦🇺 | AUD | AUD tied to commodities & China (Chapter 6). |
| **Reserve Bank of New Zealand (RBNZ)** 🇳🇿 | NZD | High-beta, commodity/risk-sensitive. |

> 🔑 The **Fed is the gravitational center** of the macro universe. Because the dollar is on one side of most major pairs and underpins global finance, *"what is the Fed doing relative to everyone else?"* is the most important macro question you can ask. Many of the biggest FX trends are really **Fed-vs-the-rest divergence** stories.

---

## 2.8 What to watch and how it affects your decisions

```
TRACK FOR EACH CURRENCY YOU TRADE:
  • Current policy rate and the EXPECTED path (hike/hold/cut cycle)
  • The next meeting date and what the market has PRICED IN
  • The bank's tone trend: getting more hawkish or dovish?
  • Real rates (nominal − inflation): ahead of or behind the curve?
  • Balance-sheet direction (QE vs QT / liquidity)

TURN IT INTO A BIAS:
  • Bank turning MORE HAWKISH vs the other currency's bank   → bullish bias for it
  • Bank turning MORE DOVISH vs the other                    → bearish bias
  • Widening favorable rate differential                     → supports the currency (carry)
  • Around meetings: expect volatility; size down or stand aside (Ch. 4 & 8)
```

> 🔑 Your macro bias for any pair starts here: **compare the two central banks' policy *paths* relative to what's priced in.** That single comparison, kept current, is the backbone of professional FX directional bias — everything in the next chapters refines it.
