# Chapter 6 — Intermarket Analysis

[← Geopolitics & Risk Sentiment](05-geopolitics-risk.md) | [Volume 6 Home](README.md) | [Next: Building Your Research Process →](07-research-process.md)

---

Currencies don't move in a vacuum. They're one piece of a single, interconnected global market alongside **bonds, stocks, and commodities.** **Intermarket analysis** studies how these markets influence each other — and it's a genuine edge, because the *other* markets often tell you what's happening to a currency *before* the currency fully reflects it. The top 1% watch the whole board, not just the FX square.

---

## 6.1 The four interconnected markets

```
        ┌─────────────┐         ┌─────────────┐
        │   BONDS     │◄───────►│ CURRENCIES  │
        │  (yields)   │         │   (FX)      │
        └──────┬──────┘         └──────┬──────┘
               │                       │
        ┌──────▼──────┐         ┌──────▼──────┐
        │   STOCKS    │◄───────►│ COMMODITIES │
        │ (equities)  │         │ (oil, gold) │
        └─────────────┘         └─────────────┘
   All linked by INTEREST RATES, GROWTH, INFLATION, and RISK SENTIMENT.
```

These four are tied together by the same macro forces you've learned (rates, growth, inflation, risk). Reading them together gives you **confirmation and early warning** for your currency views.

---

## 6.2 Bonds & yields — the closest link to FX ⭐

**Government bond yields are the most important intermarket signal for currencies**, because yields *are* interest rates — the return on holding the currency (Chapter 2).

- **Rising bond yields** (especially relative to another country's) → the currency becomes more attractive to hold → **currency tends to strengthen.**
- **Yield differentials** between two countries' bonds (e.g. US 10-year vs German 10-year) track major pairs (EUR/USD) closely. Watch the **2-year yield** especially — it's highly sensitive to expected central-bank policy.
- Yields often **lead** the currency: a shift in rate expectations shows up in bonds first, then FX follows.

### The yield curve — a recession radar
The **yield curve** plots yields across maturities (2-year to 10-year, etc.):
- **Normal (upward-sloping):** healthy growth expectations.
- **Inverted (short-term yields above long-term):** a classic **recession warning** — historically a reliable predictor of downturns, which shapes rate-cut expectations and risk sentiment.

> 🔑 **If you watch only one thing outside FX, watch bond yields and the rate differential.** They are the most direct, often-leading driver of currency direction — the bond market is effectively voting on the central-bank path in real time.

## 6.3 Commodities & the "commodity currencies" ⭐

Countries that export raw materials have currencies tied to commodity prices (the **trade/terms-of-trade channel**):

| Currency | Linked to | Relationship |
|----------|-----------|--------------|
| **CAD** (Canada) 🇨🇦 | **Oil** | Canada is a major oil exporter → oil up, CAD up (watch USD/CAD inversely) |
| **AUD** (Australia) 🇦🇺 | **Metals (iron ore, gold), China demand** | Commodity & China-growth proxy → metals/China up, AUD up |
| **NZD** (New Zealand) 🇳🇿 | **Agricultural / dairy** | Soft-commodity & risk-sensitive |
| **NOK** (Norway) 🇳🇴 | **Oil & gas** | Energy-exporter currency |

- **Oil ↑** helps exporters (CAD, NOK) and *hurts* big importers (JPY, EUR, and the trade balances of import-dependent economies) — and feeds **inflation**, looping back to central-bank policy (Chapter 2).
- **China** is the swing buyer of global commodities, so **AUD and NZD often act as liquid proxies for Chinese growth** and global risk appetite.

### Gold & the dollar
- **Gold and the USD are often (not always) inversely related** — gold is priced in dollars and is a non-yielding haven, so it tends to rise when the **dollar and real yields fall**, and when fear spikes (Chapter 5).
- Gold is a useful **risk-sentiment and real-yield barometer** even if you don't trade it.

> 🔑 If you trade CAD, you must watch **oil**; if you trade AUD/NZD, watch **metals, gold, and China/risk sentiment.** The commodity often moves first — giving you an early read on the currency.

## 6.4 Stocks & risk sentiment

Equity markets are the loudest **risk-sentiment gauge** (Chapter 5):

- **Rising stocks = risk-on** → supports high-yield/commodity currencies (AUD, NZD, EM), pressures havens (JPY especially).
- **Falling stocks = risk-off** → havens (USD, JPY, CHF) bid, risk currencies sold.
- **The VIX** (equity volatility / "fear index") is a direct risk-regime meter: spiking VIX = fear = risk-off = haven strength.
- Specific link: **USD/JPY and the Nikkei/S&P often move together** (risk-on lifts stocks and USD/JPY as the yen weakens), while the yen strengthens when stocks fall.

> 🔑 Use stocks and the VIX to confirm the **risk regime** (Ch. 5). They tell you whether the market is in "growth lens" or "fear lens" mode — which determines how currencies will interpret everything else.

## 6.5 Putting intermarket signals together

Intermarket analysis is most powerful as **confluence and early warning** — multiple markets telling the same story:

```
EXAMPLE — a bullish USD picture lining up across markets:
  ✓ US bond yields RISING (esp. vs other countries) → USD yield advantage
  ✓ Yield differential widening in the USD's favor
  ✓ Risk-OFF tone: stocks soft, VIX up → haven demand for USD
  ✓ Gold under pressure (strong USD / high real yields)
  → Multiple independent markets confirm USD strength → high-conviction bias.

EXAMPLE — a bullish CAD picture:
  ✓ Oil prices breaking higher
  ✓ Risk-ON (stocks up, VIX low) → supports commodity FX
  ✓ BoC turning more hawkish vs the Fed (Ch. 2)
  → Oil + risk-on + rate path all favor CAD → strong USD/CAD-short thesis.
```

When bonds, commodities, stocks, *and* the rate story all point the same way, you have a **multi-market confluence** thesis far stronger than any single signal — the macro equivalent of the technical confluence in Volume 3.

> ⚠️ **Correlations shift over time.** These relationships are tendencies, not laws — they strengthen and weaken with the regime (e.g. the gold/USD inverse can break when both are bid in a panic). Use intermarket links as *evidence and context*, re-checked against the current regime, not as mechanical rules.

## 6.6 Chapter summary

```
• Currencies are one piece of an interconnected board: BONDS, STOCKS, COMMODITIES, FX —
  all linked by rates, growth, inflation, and risk sentiment.
• BONDS/YIELDS are the closest link: rising relative yields → stronger currency; yield
  DIFFERENTIALS track major pairs; the 2-year is policy-sensitive; an INVERTED curve warns
  of recession. Watch this above all else outside FX.
• COMMODITY CURRENCIES: CAD↔oil, AUD↔metals/China, NZD↔soft commodities, NOK↔energy. Oil up
  helps exporters, hurts importers, feeds inflation. AUD/NZD = China/risk proxies.
• GOLD is often inversely related to USD/real yields and is a haven/real-yield barometer.
• STOCKS & the VIX are the loudest RISK-SENTIMENT gauge (risk-on helps risk FX & USD/JPY;
  risk-off helps havens).
• Best use: multi-market CONFLUENCE & early warning — when yields, commodities, stocks, and
  the rate story agree, conviction is high. But correlations SHIFT with the regime — context, not rules.
```
