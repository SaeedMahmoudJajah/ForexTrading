# Chapter 4 — The Expectations Game

[← Economic Indicators](03-economic-indicators.md) | [Volume 6 Home](README.md) | [Next: Geopolitics & Risk Sentiment →](05-geopolitics-risk.md)

---

This chapter explains the single most counterintuitive truth in macro trading — the one that makes beginners say *"the data was great, why did the currency fall?!"* Markets don't trade the news; they trade the **surprise** relative to what was already expected. Master this and you'll stop being blindsided and start understanding why prices *really* move.

---

## 4.1 Markets are discounting machines

A market price is a **real-time consensus forecast of the future.** Everything that is *known and expected* is already baked into the price. Thousands of professionals have already positioned for what they believe will happen.

Therefore:
- If reality arrives **exactly as expected**, there's little reason for the price to move — it was already there.
- The price moves when reality **differs from expectation** — the **surprise** forces everyone to re-price.

```
PRICE TODAY  =  the market's best guess of the future, already positioned for

PRICE MOVE   =  reaction to the GAP between what happens and what was expected
             =  the "SURPRISE"  (also called the "delta" or the "beat/miss")
```

> 🔑 **You are not trading events. You are trading deviations from expectations.** This is why "great" news can crash a currency (it was expected to be even *greater*) and "bad" news can rally it (it was expected to be *worse*). Re-read that until it's instinct.

---

## 4.2 The three numbers on every data release

Every economic release comes with three figures — learn to read them as a set:

| Number | Meaning |
|--------|---------|
| **Previous** | Last period's reading (the baseline / trend) |
| **Forecast (Consensus)** | What economists collectively *expect* this time — **this is what's priced in** |
| **Actual** | The number that just printed |

**The move is driven by Actual vs Forecast:**
- **Actual > Forecast** (a "beat") on growth/inflation/jobs → typically **hawkish surprise** → currency up.
- **Actual < Forecast** (a "miss") → typically **dovish surprise** → currency down.
- **Actual ≈ Forecast** → muted reaction; the market looks past it to the next catalyst.

> 🔑 Before any release, the only question that matters is: *"what's the consensus, and what would surprise the market?"* If you don't know what was expected, you cannot interpret what the number means.

---

## 4.3 "Buy the rumor, sell the fact"

The most famous expression of the expectations game. Because markets price in *anticipated* events ahead of time:

- A currency often **rallies in the run-up** to an expected positive event (the "rumor" — e.g. an anticipated rate hike) as traders position for it.
- Then, when the event actually happens (the "fact"), those traders **take profit**, and the currency **falls** *despite the positive news* — because the news is now "out" and fully priced.

```
   price
     │            ╱‾‾‾●  ← event happens (the "fact") — and price FALLS
     │          ╱        traders who "bought the rumor" now SELL it
     │        ╱
     │      ╱   ← anticipation rally ("buying the rumor")
     │ ___╱
     └─────────────────────── time
         expected good news approaches → arrives
```

This is why you'll see a currency drop on a rate *hike* it received, or a stock sell off on great earnings. **The reaction depends entirely on positioning vs expectation, not on the headline.**

> 🔑 When everyone already expects something and is positioned for it, the *actual* event is often a "sell the fact" reversal. Ask: *"is this already in the price, and is everyone already on this side?"* Extreme one-sided positioning is fragile.

---

## 4.4 The economic calendar — your essential tool

The **economic calendar** (free on ForexFactory, Investing.com, your broker, etc.) lists every upcoming release with its time, impact rating, previous, forecast, and (when out) actual. It is the macro trader's daily map.

**How to use it:**
```
EVERY DAY before trading:
  □ Scan for HIGH-IMPACT (red) events on your currencies today/this week
  □ Note the exact TIME and the FORECAST (what's priced in)
  □ Mark central-bank meetings & speeches — the biggest movers
  □ Plan AROUND them: which events you'll avoid, which you'll trade after

KEY RECURRING HIGH-IMPACT EVENTS TO KNOW:
  • Central-bank rate decisions & press conferences (biggest)
  • CPI / inflation releases
  • NFP / jobs report (1st Friday, US)
  • GDP, PMIs/ISM, retail sales
```

> ⚠️ **Never get blindsided.** Being long into a surprise central-bank decision you didn't know was scheduled is an unforced error. Checking the calendar daily is non-negotiable professional hygiene (it's in your Vol 1 & Vol 5 routines for a reason).

---

## 4.5 How the pros use expectations to their advantage

- **Trade the regime and the trend, not the spike.** The first seconds after a release are chaotic — wide spreads, slippage, whipsaws. Pros more often **wait for the dust to settle** and trade the *clearer directional move* that follows once the market digests the surprise (Chapter 8).
- **Fade extreme positioning.** When sentiment and positioning are wildly one-sided into an event, the "priced-in" risk is asymmetric — a small disappointment can cause an outsized reversal.
- **Watch the *reaction*, not just the number.** A *bullish* number that fails to lift the currency (or a bearish one that fails to sink it) reveals the market's true lean — often a powerful tell about what's already priced and where the surprise risk lies.
- **Track the shifting consensus.** Rate-expectation tools and economist forecasts show how the "priced-in" path evolves between meetings; trading *that drift* is core macro.

> 🔑 **The market's *reaction* to news is more informative than the news itself.** When a currency won't fall on bad news, the bad news was already priced and the path of least resistance may be *up*. Reading reactions is an edge most retail traders never develop.

---

## 4.6 Revisions and the "second look"

- **Data gets revised.** Initial GDP/jobs prints are estimates, later revised. A strong initial number that's later revised down can reverse a move. Pros weight the *trend across revisions*, not a single print.
- **The details beneath the headline matter.** A jobs report can beat on headline but disappoint on *wages*; markets often trade the more policy-relevant internal (Chapter 3). Read past the headline number to the components the central bank cares about.

---

## 4.7 Chapter summary

```
• Markets are DISCOUNTING MACHINES — price already reflects everything known/expected.
• Price moves on the SURPRISE = (Actual − Forecast/consensus), not on the raw news.
• Read every release as a set: PREVIOUS (trend), FORECAST (priced in), ACTUAL (result).
  Beat = hawkish surprise (currency up); miss = dovish (down); inline = muted.
• "BUY THE RUMOR, SELL THE FACT": expected events are priced ahead, then reverse on the
  news as positioned traders take profit. Always ask "is this already in the price?"
• The ECONOMIC CALENDAR is daily non-negotiable hygiene — know the time, forecast, and
  high-impact events; plan around them; never get blindsided.
• Pros trade the post-release TREND not the spike, fade extreme positioning, and read the
  market's REACTION (a non-reaction to news is a powerful tell).
• Mind revisions and sub-components — the headline isn't the whole story.
```
